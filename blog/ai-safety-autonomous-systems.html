Why AI Safety Matters for Autonomous Systems

Yu Li

As large foundation models are increasingly deployed in safety-critical settings — from self-driving cars to semiconductor manufacturing — understanding and guaranteeing their reliability becomes essential. This is not merely an academic concern: failures in these systems can have real-world consequences ranging from traffic accidents to manufacturing defects that affect millions of chips.

In our research group at Zhejiang University, we explore how AI systems can fail, be attacked, or produce unreliable outputs — and more importantly, how to make them robust. Here I want to share some key perspectives on why AI safety is one of the most pressing research challenges of our time.

The Gap Between Capability and Reliability

Modern LLMs and generative models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities. Yet capability does not imply reliability. A model that achieves 99% accuracy on a benchmark may still fail catastrophically on edge cases that matter most in deployment. In autonomous driving, for instance, these edge cases are exactly the scenarios where safety is most critical.

Adversarial Threats Are Real

Our work has shown that state-of-the-art models — including large vision-language models — remain vulnerable to carefully crafted adversarial inputs. In physical-world settings, such attacks can manifest as adversarial camouflage on vehicles or subtle perturbations in sensor data. Defending against these threats requires a deep understanding of model internals, not just surface-level patching.

The IC Domain Adds a New Dimension

AI is now being applied across the semiconductor design and manufacturing pipeline — from EDA tools to computational lithography. This creates a new class of reliability challenges: what happens when an AI-assisted EDA tool produces a subtly flawed netlist, or when a lithography simulation model overfits to training data and misses critical process variations?

These questions motivate much of our ongoing research, and I look forward to sharing more specific findings in future posts.
